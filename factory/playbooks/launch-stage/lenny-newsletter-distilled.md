# Lenny Rachitsky Newsletter — Frameworks Distilled

Lenny's Newsletter is the most-cited PM/growth source for 2024-2026. Below are the frameworks worth knowing — each in 100-200 words, with a link out to the source.

## 1. PMF Engine (Rahul Vohra / Superhuman)
Use the Sean Ellis survey ("how would you feel if you could no longer use ___"), but go further:
- Segment "very disappointed" users by persona — find the sweet spot
- For "somewhat disappointed" users, ask "what's missing?" — that's your roadmap
- Build a "PMF engine" — recurring survey + segment analysis + roadmap loop

[See `mvp-stage/sean-ellis-survey.md` for the implementation detail.]

## 2. The 1-1-3 framework for product strategy
1 vision → 1 strategy → 3 bets. Forces focus. Anything you're working on that doesn't ladder up to one of the 3 bets is a distraction. Revisit quarterly.

## 3. Activation rate framework
Define activation as "the moment a user gets value once." Measure: % of signups who activate within 24 hours. Indie SaaS benchmark: 25-50%. Below 20% = onboarding broken. Above 60% = strong product-market fit signal.

## 4. The "build vs buy" decision matrix
Buy if: the function is non-core, exists as commodity, cheaper than your time. Build if: it's a moat, customizable to your differentiation, or you'd be vendor-locked. Most indie founders over-build (NIH syndrome) — default to buy at MVP stage.

## 5. The "wedge" strategy
Find the niche where you can be 10x better than alternatives. Win there completely. Then expand outward — not horizontally (different jobs) but adjacently (related jobs for the same customer). Most product death comes from premature horizontal expansion.

## 6. The "frequency × severity" prioritization
For any user pain, rate: frequency (daily/weekly/monthly) × severity (annoying/costly/blocking). Build for **daily × blocking** first. Defer daily × annoying. Skip monthly × annoying.

## 7. The growth model spreadsheet
Build a simple spreadsheet projecting MRR 12 months out, with inputs: signups/week, activation rate, conversion rate, ARPU, churn. Sensitivity-test inputs. Reveals: what's the lever that matters most? Often it's retention, not acquisition.

## 8. The "stakeholder map" for B2B sales
For each prospect, identify: champion (will push internally), economic buyer (signs the check), user (will use the product), influencer (could veto), gatekeeper (procurement). Tailor outreach to each role. Most lost B2B deals lose at "economic buyer" stage because the champion couldn't sell up.

## 9. Onboarding: the "aha moment" framework
Map the user's journey from signup → first value moment ("aha"). Measure time-to-aha. Aggressively reduce friction in every step. Most products have 5-10 unnecessary steps between signup and aha.

## 10. The "cold start" problem (marketplaces)
Two-sided marketplaces face cold start: empty side scares away the other. Solutions in order of preference: (1) hand-curate supply (founder-led), (2) atomic network (start in one tiny geo / niche), (3) pay for supply (last resort, doesn't scale). Don't try to launch both sides simultaneously.

## 11. Cohort retention curves (Brian Balfour)
Plot retention by signup cohort. A "smile" curve (drops, then rises) = users return after a gap. A "flat tail" = PMF. A "decay" = leaky bucket. The shape matters more than the absolute %.

## 12. The "founder mode" debate
Brian Chesky / Paul Graham articulated: founders need to be deeply in details longer than business school says. Detail-mode at small scale = right; at large scale = bottleneck. The transition point is around employee 30-50. At indie/portfolio scale: stay in founder mode but use automation as your "team."
