# Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

**Source:** Clayton Christensen (HBR articles) + Tony Ulwick's *Outcome-Driven Innovation* — refined further by Bob Moesta and Chris Spiek.

## The framework in one paragraph

People don't buy products; they "hire" products to do a job for them. The job is a progress the customer is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Understanding the job — its functional, emotional, and social dimensions — reveals why customers choose what they choose, far better than demographic or feature-based analysis. The famous example: people don't buy a quarter-inch drill; they hire it to make a quarter-inch hole. (And even that's wrong — they actually hire it to hang a picture, which is why a "no-drill picture-hanging strip" is a competing product.)

## When to use it

- **Idea stage**: framing the problem you're solving
- **MVP stage**: prioritizing features that serve the job
- **Launch stage**: positioning and messaging
- **Scale stage**: spotting new jobs adjacent to the one you serve

## Step-by-step

### 1. Identify the job
What progress is the customer trying to make? Job statements follow this format:
> When <situation>, I want to <motivation>, so I can <outcome>.

Example:
> When I'm reviewing customer interview notes, I want to identify recurring patterns across conversations, so I can decide which feature to prioritize next.

### 2. Identify the dimensions of the job
- **Functional**: the practical task (transcribe interviews, find patterns)
- **Emotional**: the feeling sought (confidence in the decision, less anxiety)
- **Social**: how they want to be perceived (smart, data-driven, customer-centric)

Most products only serve the functional dimension and miss the emotional and social — that's where competitors win.

### 3. Identify the competing alternatives
What does the customer currently "hire" to do this job? Including:
- Existing tools
- Non-tool workarounds (spreadsheets, "doing it manually")
- Doing nothing

### 4. Map the forces of progress (Moesta-Spiek "Four Forces")
For why someone switches:
- **Push of current situation**: what's bad now
- **Pull of new solution**: what looks better
- **Anxiety of new solution**: what could go wrong
- **Habit of current solution**: inertia

A successful product maximizes push + pull, minimizes anxiety + habit.

## Templates / questions

### Job interview questions (different from Mom Test interviews; you can combine)
1. "Walk me through the last time you did <job>. Start from when you first realized you needed to do it."
2. "What were you doing right before you started?"
3. "What did you try first? Why?"
4. "If you couldn't have used <current tool>, what would you have done?"
5. "What was hard? What was surprising?"
6. "How did it end? Did you feel done?"

## Common failure modes

- **Defining the job too narrowly.** "Send an email" is a feature, not a job. "Get my team aligned on a decision by Friday" is a job.
- **Defining the job too broadly.** "Run a business" is not a job, it's life.
- **Ignoring emotional / social dimensions.** Functional-only positioning gets out-positioned.
- **Confusing the job with the persona.** Jobs are situational. The same person hires different products for different jobs.
