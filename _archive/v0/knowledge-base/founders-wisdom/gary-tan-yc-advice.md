# Gary Tan / YC — Era-Specific Advice (2024-2026)

The patterns Gary Tan (YC president) has been hammering on since taking the role.

## The framework in one paragraph

Gary Tan's YC has emphasized AI-native company building, founder mode (deep founder involvement longer), and that the bar for AI products is execution-not-novelty. The era's specific advice: focus on talking to users, ship fast, get to default-alive, and remember that distribution is the moat — better products with better distribution beat better products without.

## Repeated themes

### "Talk to users every week"
Gary repeats this so much it's a meme. The right cadence: 5+ user conversations per week, every week, for the first year minimum.

### "Make something people want"
The eternal YC line. Gary's version: in 2026 with AI, the easier-than-ever building means people are shipping things they don't want — discipline matters more, not less.

### "Default-alive"
Be on a trajectory where current burn + current growth = profitability before runway ends. Default-dead = need to raise or pivot. For indie products at $100K ARR: default-alive is the goal, not optional.

### "Founder mode"
Brian Chesky's concept, embraced by Gary. Founders should stay deep in detail longer than business school says. The default org chart with executives autonomous-mode-ing things into the ground is wrong for startups.

### "The bar for AI is execution"
Every AI idea has 50 versions live. The differentiator isn't novelty — it's: better onboarding, more reliable backend, better support, faster iteration, deeper integration, lower price, better trust. Boring stuff wins.

### "Distribution is the moat"
Better product loses to better distribution every time. Build the distribution motion alongside the product, not after. This is especially true post-2023 when AI products commoditize fast.

### "Build with AI as infrastructure"
Use AI as the cofounder you don't have. Research, code, ops, marketing — all amplified. The lean 10-person unicorn is the new playbook.

## Specific 2025-2026 patterns

### "Wedge then expand"
Pick a niche. Win 10x better in it. Then expand. Don't horizontal-platform on day 1.

### "Sell before you build (B2B)"
Get 5 LOIs / paid pilots BEFORE writing production code. Validates demand, funds the build, anchors the design.

### "Ship every week"
Velocity is the metric. Weekly releases keep the team learning. Slow shipping = lost momentum + wrong product.

### "Charge from day 1"
Free products attract the wrong users. Even $5/mo filters for "I have a real need." Free tiers come later, deliberately.

### "B2B SaaS for unsexy industries"
The best $100K-$10M ARR opportunities are in vertical SaaS for industries the SF / VC crowd ignores: trucking, legal, healthcare admin, construction, accounting. Less competition, customers who pay because the alternative is worse.

### "The 10-person unicorn"
Achievable in 2026 with AI infrastructure. Each function gets an AI cofounder. Headcount stays low; revenue and impact scale. This factory is built on the same premise — one founder, 10 products, AI orchestrating the operational work.

## YC application bar (informal)

For founders considering YC (not directly relevant for an indie portfolio, but signal-rich):
- Solo founder is now acceptable (was harder pre-2022)
- Need: traction OR uniquely qualified founder OR clearly differentiated insight
- "Tell us about a time you hacked a system" question is real
- AI startups need: not just "AI for X" but a real distribution / wedge / data angle
- B2B vertical SaaS strongly preferred over horizontal infrastructure plays in 2026

## When NOT to apply YC thinking

- Lifestyle business: most YC frameworks assume venture trajectory
- Geo arbitrage / nomad SaaS: YC assumes US-incorporated, US-customer-base focus
- Acquired-channel-only businesses (drop-shipping etc.): different playbook entirely
