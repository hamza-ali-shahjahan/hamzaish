# Paul Graham — Essays Worth Internalizing

The PG essays that most directly apply to running a startup factory.

## The framework in one paragraph

PG's essays cluster around a small number of repeated insights: ideas come from noticing what you'd want yourself, not by brainstorming; the work that scales doesn't feel like the work that scales (do things that don't scale); growth is the only metric that matters when nothing else is clear; the founders who win are the ones who keep going through the trough that everyone hits.

## The essential 10

### 1. "How to Get Startup Ideas" (2012)
- The best ideas come from problems you have or notice
- "Live in the future, then build what's missing" — be at the frontier of some domain, build for that frontier
- Bad ideas are usually too vague; sharpen until specific
- Beware "ideas that sound good in a pitch but don't survive customer contact"

### 2. "Do Things That Don't Scale" (2013)
- Recruit users one at a time, by hand, for as long as you can
- "Sufficiently determined founders can make it happen" — manual effort that wouldn't survive at 1M users is correct at 10 users
- Stripe famously installed their early customers' code themselves
- The unsustainable work is the actual work in the early stage

### 3. "Schlep Blindness" (2012)
- The best startup ideas are often the ones you avoid because they look like too much work
- Stripe avoided by people who didn't want to deal with payment compliance
- The schlep itself is the moat — competitors avoided the same hard work

### 4. "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" (2009)
- Makers need 4-hour blocks
- Managers can context-switch
- A single meeting can blow up a maker's day
- For solo founders running a portfolio: structure the day in maker-blocks; batch all manager-work

### 5. "How to Make Wealth" (2004)
- Wealth ≠ money. Wealth = what people want.
- Startups compress your career: 10 years of 9-5 work into 4 years of 100-hour weeks
- Measurement + leverage = wealth creation
- Solo / small team is more efficient than big team for early-stage

### 6. "The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn" (2006)
- Release early
- Keep pumping out features
- Make users happy
- Fear the right things (running out of money, wrong direction)
- Commitment is a self-fulfilling prophecy

### 7. "Beating the Averages" (2003)
- Pick technology choices that give you leverage (LISP for Viaweb; Claude+Next.js for 2026 indie SaaS)
- Don't pick technology because it's safe — pick because it's powerful for your problem

### 8. "How to Start a Startup" (2005, updated)
- Need: good people, good product, low expenses
- Solo founders work (PG was originally skeptical; updated his view based on data)
- Get acquired or grow profitable — those are the two outcomes

### 9. "Why to Start a Startup in a Bad Economy" (2008)
- Bad economy = less noise, less competition for hiring, more talent available
- Startups are cheap to start; the bad economy doesn't change that
- Macros don't matter as much as you think for early-stage

### 10. "Cities and Ambition" (2008)
- The place you live sends you a message about what to care about
- For indie / remote founders: deliberately choose the inputs (Twitter, conferences, communities) since you're not in SF/NYC
- The factory analog: the inputs into this brain (knowledge base, X follows, podcasts) shape what gets built

## PG patterns to recognize and apply

### "The thing that doesn't scale is the work"
At 0-100 customers, the founder doing manual onboarding IS the work. Don't try to scale prematurely (per the playbook's premature-scaling warning).

### "Make something people want"
Famous YC slogan, originally PG. Not "make something people will use if marketing convinces them" — make something they want enough to demand. PMF.

### "If you're not embarrassed by your first version, you launched too late"
Reid Hoffman line that PG endorses. The first version should be embarrassingly minimal.

### "Be the user"
Best products come from solving your own problem. Wrong problems get over-engineered; right problems get the right fix.

### "Disagree with yourself"
PG's mental tool: argue both sides. Sharpens thinking. (We've baked this into `agents/idea/devils-advocate/`.)

## When NOT to follow PG

PG essays are biased toward venture-backed, SF-network startups. For $100K-ARR indie products targeted here, adjust:
- "Growth is the only metric" — true for venture; for indie, profitability + sustainable growth both matter
- "Hire fast" — false for indie / portfolio. Headcount is the enemy.
- "Raise capital" — false for indie; default-alive bootstrap is the play
