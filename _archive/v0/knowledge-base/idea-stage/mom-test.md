# The Mom Test

**Source:** *The Mom Test* by Rob Fitzpatrick (2013). The most-cited customer-interview methodology in indie-startup circles.

## The framework in one paragraph

The Mom Test is a set of three rules for customer conversations: (1) Talk about their **life**, not your **idea**. (2) Ask about **specifics in the past**, not generics about the future. (3) Talk less, listen more. The name comes from the observation that your mom will validate any idea, no matter how bad — because the questions you ask her are leading. Good questions about user behavior reveal truth even from people biased toward telling you what you want to hear.

## When to use it

- Every customer discovery interview at the **Idea** stage
- User research interviews at any stage where you're trying to learn about behavior
- Always when you're about to ask "would you use a product that...?" — that's a Mom Test violation

## The three rules

### 1. Talk about their life, not your idea
- ❌ "Do you think this is a good idea?"
- ✅ "How do you currently handle X?"

The idea is irrelevant in a discovery interview. You're learning about their problem. The product comes later.

### 2. Ask about specific past behavior, not generic future intent
- ❌ "Would you pay for a tool that does Y?"
- ✅ "Tell me about the last time you tried to do Y. What did you use? How long did it take?"

People are bad at predicting their own future behavior. They are excellent at describing what they actually did.

### 3. Talk less, listen more
- ❌ Talking for 30% of the call
- ✅ Talking for ≤ 15% of the call

If you're describing your product, you're not learning. The interviewee should be doing most of the work.

## Step-by-step interview structure (15 min)

1. **Warm-up (2 min)** — "Tell me about your role / company / typical week"
2. **Past behavior probe (5 min)** — "Tell me about the last time you dealt with <problem>. Walk me through it."
3. **Current solution (3 min)** — "What did you try? What worked? What didn't?"
4. **Workaround cost (2 min)** — "How much time / money / pain does this currently cost you?"
5. **Magic wand (2 min)** — "If you could wave a wand, how would this look?"
6. **Close (1 min)** — "Who else has this problem? Mind connecting me?"

## Bad questions to strike out of any interview script

- "Do you think it's a good idea?"
- "Would you use a product that did X?"
- "Would you pay $Y for it?"
- "Wouldn't it be cool if...?"
- Anything starting with "Imagine that..."

## Signals of a good interview

- **They told you a specific story** (with names, dates, dollar amounts)
- **They mentioned current workarounds** (proving the problem exists)
- **They got animated about the pain** (proves it matters)
- **They referred you to someone else** (the most reliable validation signal)
- **They asked to try the product** (after the interview, unprompted — extremely strong signal)

## Signals of a bad interview

- You did most of the talking
- They were polite and supportive throughout (probably the Mom Test failing)
- They said "yes, that would be cool" to every feature
- They never mentioned a current workaround
- They didn't refer you to anyone else

## Common failure modes

- **Compliments are worthless.** "Wow that's a cool idea" tells you nothing.
- **Hypothetical answers are worthless.** "I'd totally use that" predicts nothing.
- **The founder is selling, not learning.** If you find yourself defending the idea, you've failed.
- **The user is the friend, not the customer.** Friends and family give bad data because they want to support you.
