# YC Startup School — Distilled Notes (Gary Tan era, 2024–2026)

The set of beliefs YC has been hammering on consistently in the current era. Useful as a sanity-check.

## Core beliefs

### 1. Make something people want
The single most repeated YC line. Means: validation > engineering, distribution > polish.

### 2. Talk to users
- 5+ per week, every week, for the first year minimum
- Founder-led, not delegated to user research
- Mom Test rules (see `mom-test.md`)

### 3. Build → measure → learn
- Ship a minimal version weekly
- Measure with one north-star metric (not a dashboard)
- Adjust based on what users do, not what they say

### 4. Default-alive vs default-dead
- "Default-alive" = your current trajectory hits profitability before runway dies
- "Default-dead" = you need to raise or pivot before runway dies
- For indie products at $100K ARR target: default-alive is the goal, not optional

### 5. Founder-market fit
- Pick problems you've experienced or have unique access to
- Don't pick problems you'd need to "study" to understand the customer

### 6. Talk to one user at a time. Build for one user at a time.
Solve one user's problem 10x better before generalizing. The generalization comes from the second user telling you "I had this same problem but..." — not from imagining.

### 7. The 1,000 true fans rule
For indie SaaS at $100/mo, 1,000 customers = $1.2M ARR. For $30/mo, 3,333 customers = $1.2M. The math works at almost every price point — what's hard is reaching them.

## Top advice for AI-native era

### "Wedge then expand"
Pick a tiny niche your product is 10x better in. Win there completely. Then expand outward. Don't try to be a horizontal platform on day 1.

### "Sell before you build"
For B2B: get 5 letters-of-intent or paid pilots before writing production code.

### "The bar for AI products is execution, not novelty"
Every AI product idea has 50 versions live. The winners are the ones that execute the boring stuff (auth, billing, support, reliability) better than competitors.

### "Distribution is the moat"
Better product loses to better distribution every time. Build the distribution motion alongside the product, not after.

### "Don't raise too early"
For most indie/small SaaS: don't raise. For VC-backable: raise after PMF, not before.

## YC red flags they reject for

- Founders who haven't talked to users
- Markets that are too small (< $1B TAM for venture; not relevant for indie)
- Solutions in search of a problem
- "We're like X for Y" without understanding why X doesn't already do Y
- Co-founders who don't trust each other (different ramifications for solo founders — see Naval, Pieter Levels for solo-founder evidence)
- No "why now" — the problem has existed for years and no one has solved it, so why now?

## Useful YC frameworks

### The 7-question PMF check
1. Are users coming back?
2. Are users telling friends?
3. Are users willing to pay?
4. Can you reach more users cost-effectively?
5. Is the cost structure sustainable?
6. Do you (founder) want to do this for 10 years?
7. Is the market growing?

If 5+ "yes" you have PMF. If 4 or fewer: not yet.

### The "if you stopped working on it tomorrow" test
If you stopped: would your users be devastated? If yes — PMF. If they'd shrug and find a substitute in a day — not yet.
