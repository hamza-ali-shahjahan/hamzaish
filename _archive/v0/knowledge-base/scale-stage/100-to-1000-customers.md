# 100 → 1000 Customers

## The framework in one paragraph

The play from 100 to 1000 customers is fundamentally different from 0 → 100. The 0 → 100 was manual. The 100 → 1000 is **systematic**: you've identified which 1-2 channels work, and you scale them with process, content, and modest automation. The founder shifts from "in every conversation" to "designs the systems that have the conversations." Most products that hit 100 fail to reach 1000 because they keep doing the 0 → 100 playbook and burn out — or because they try to scale before retention is solid.

## Pre-requisites (before attempting this stage)

- ✅ Sean Ellis ≥40% on your active users (or equivalent PMF signal)
- ✅ M3 retention ≥ category benchmark (see `mvp-stage/measurement-framework.md`)
- ✅ CAC < (LTV / 3) on your best channel
- ✅ One channel works repeatably (not "we got 10 customers from Twitter — let's scale Twitter" but "we send 100 cold emails/week and 5 convert, and we know which subject line wins")

If any of these is missing — stop. Fix retention or channel proof before scaling.

## The systematic-channel play

By customer 100, you should know 1-2 channels that work. Each gets a system:

### If cold outreach works
- Hire a VA or use Apollo + Smartlead to scale to 200-500 sends/week
- Founder still writes the templates; VA executes the volume
- Track per-template conversion; rotate to keep response rates up
- Customer #100-#500 likely come mostly from this

### If SEO works
- Scale to 2-4 posts/month (started 1/month)
- Each post: target a keyword cluster, fully optimized, internally linked
- Build link velocity through guest posts + partnerships
- Customer #100-#500 trickle in over 6-12 months (slower but compounding)

### If community works
- Founder presence in 3-5 niche communities (IH, niche subreddits, Slack groups)
- Genuinely helpful — don't just promote
- Customer #100-#500 come from being known in the community

### If referrals work
- Add an explicit referral mechanism (Rewardful / Tolt / build your own)
- Reward both sides (referrer + referee)
- Track viral coefficient — if K > 0.3, this becomes your dominant channel

### If partnerships work
- Identify 5-10 adjacent tools your users also use
- Pitch integrations + co-marketing
- Customer #100-#500 come from partner traffic

## The founder shift

By customer 100, you're spending too much time on:
- Support (every ticket goes to you)
- Demo calls (every prospect wants a 30-min demo)
- Onboarding (you walk every new user through)
- Cold outreach (you send every email manually)

By customer 300, none of these should be founder-only anymore. Either:
- **Automate** (in-app onboarding, async docs, support bot for L1)
- **Delegate** (VA for outreach scheduling, support triage)
- **Eliminate** (drop the 30-min demo for low-fit prospects)

This shift is **uncomfortable**. You feel the quality drop in support replies, the cold outreach loses some personalization, the onboarding becomes slightly less magical. That's fine. Quality drops 10% but volume scales 5x — net positive.

## What happens to MRR

Typical pattern for $100K-ARR target:
- Customer 100: $2K-$5K MRR (early customers often discounted)
- Customer 300: $7K-$15K MRR (pricing has been raised; better mix)
- Customer 500: $15K-$30K MRR
- Customer 1000: $30K-$80K MRR (mix of self-serve + small team plans)

To hit $100K ARR ($8.3K MRR), 200-400 paying customers at $30-50 ARPU. Or 80-160 at $50-100. Or 30-60 at $150-250.

## The "what to NOT do" list at this stage

- **Don't pursue enterprise prematurely.** Big logos sound good but eat founder time at 10x SMB.
- **Don't expand to a new market.** Different users = different product needs. Burns focus.
- **Don't add a free tier.** If you didn't have one, the answer is almost certainly no.
- **Don't redesign the product.** Optimize what works; don't disrupt.
- **Don't hire a "growth person" before $30K MRR.** Founder still owns growth strategy.
- **Don't lower prices to grow faster.** Counter-productive.

## When to consider the next stage (1000 → 10,000)

When you're at $30-50K MRR with stable retention and you have a clear sense of which segment is the wedge for the next 10x. That's a different playbook — see Reforge / Brian Balfour material.

## Common failure modes

- **Founder burnout at customer 200-400.** Was doing everything for too long. Solution: forced delegation (`agents/scale/support-triage`, `workflows/`).
- **Retention quietly degrades.** New cohorts behave worse than early ones (lower-fit users from scaled acquisition). Solution: tighten targeting; raise prices.
- **Churn surfaces hidden.** Annual contracts mask early churn until renewal year. Solution: weekly cohort tracking, not just MRR.
- **Premature feature work.** Building new things instead of optimizing what works.
- **Premature international expansion.** Adds complexity (currency, tax, GDPR, localization) without proportionally more revenue.
